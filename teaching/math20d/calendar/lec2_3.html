Lecture 2.3 Overview

  • Existence and Uniqueness, The Wronskian. It's worth revisiting this idea. Theorem 3.2.1 tells us that a unique solution to the initial value problem always exists. Theorems 3.2.2 and 3.2.3 suggest a way to find this solution. First, we try to find any two solutions of the differential equation (we're not worried about initial conditions yet). So for the real, distinct root case these are \[ y_1(t) = e^{r_1 t}, \quad y_2(t) = e^{r_2 t} \] where \(r_1,r_2\) are the roots which by assumption are real and \(r_1 \ne r_2\). Theorem 3.2.2 now tells us that for any constants \(C_1,C_2\), we can construct the new solution \[ y(t) = C_1 y_1(t) + C_2 y_2(t). \] Next, Theorem 3.2.3 tells us that if the Wronskian \(W(y_1,y_2) (t_0) \ne 0\), then for any initial conditions \(y_0, y_0'\), the unique solution to the initial value problem can be written as \(y(t) = C_1 y_1(t) + C y_2(t)\). In other words, since we already know \(y\) is a solution to the differential equation, the theorem tells us that we can solve for \(C_1,C_2\) thus satisfying the intial conditions. The uniqueness part of Theorem 3.2.1 means that this is now the solution.
  • Complex Roots of the Characteristic Equation Sometimes the roots of the characteristic equation are complex. In this case, we can construct (by means of Euler's formula) two solutions, \begin{align*} u_1(t) &= e^{(\lambda + i \mu)t} = e^{\lambda t} (\cos \mu t + i \sin \mu t,\\ u_2(t) &= e^{(\lambda - i \mu)t} = e^{\lambda t} (\cos \mu t - i \sin \mu t,\\ \end{align*} where the roots are \[ r_1 = \lambda + i \mu, r_2 = \lambda - i \mu \] which occur in complex conjugate pairs.

    The problem here is that these solutions are complex valued! By taking the real and imaginary parts of either \(y_1\) or \(y_2\) we can obtain the following real solutions \[ y_1(t) = e^{\lambda t} \cos \mu t, \quad y_2(t) = e^{\lambda t} \sin \mu t. \] You can check that the Wronskian is not zero for any \(t\) and so we have a fundamental set of solutions \(\{y_1,y_2\}\). That is, the general solution is \[ y(t) = e^{\lambda t} (C_1 \cos \mu t + C_2 \sin \mu t) \] which means that for any initial conditions \(y_0,y_0'\) we can find \(C_1, C_2\) to satsify the initial conditions.

  • Real, repeated roots of the Characteristic Equation. Here the characteristic equation has a repeated root \(r\) and so one solution is given by \(y_1(t) = e^{rt}\) but we don't get a second solution. To find a second solution, use variation of parameters to find a second solution
\[ y_2(t) = t e^{rt}. \]

Again, you can check that the Wronskian is non-zero so that \(e^{rt}, te^{rt}\) is a fundamental set of solutions.

Lecture 2.3 Notes

Existence and Uniqueness, The Wronskian.

The important things to remember here are

  • a unique solution always exists,
  • given solutions of the differential equation \(y_1,y_2\) a linear combination is again a solution,
  • if the Wronskian \(W(y_1,y_2)\) is non-zero then the general solution is \(C_1 y_1 + C_2 y_2\) which means we can always solve for any initial conditions. We also say that \(\{y_1,y_2\}\) is a fundamental set of solutions.

Complex Roots of the Characteristic Equation.

  • Euler's formula : \(e^{x + i y} = e^x(\cos y + i \sin y\)
  • \(i\) is a constant! Thus \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial t} (i f(t)) = i \frac{\partial}{\partial t} (f(t)). \]

Real, repeated roots of the Characteristic Equation.

  • We will talk about how to get from the guess \(y_2(t) = v(t) e^{rt}\) to finding that \(v(t) = t\) on Monday. The idea is to assume \(y_2\) is a solution and see what conditions this imposes on \(v\) and then solve for \(v\).
