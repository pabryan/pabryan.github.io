Lecture 3.1 Overview

  • Convergence of Taylor Polynomials

We would like to know when the Taylor polynomials converge to \(f(x)\) as \(n \to \infty\). This is the same as saying that \(f\) is analytic. The Taylor polynomials are the \(n\)-th partial sums of a series.

  • Lagrange Remainder Theorem

The remainder allows us to determine when a function is analytic. This is true if and only if the remainder converges to \(0\) as \(n\to \infty\). The Lagrange remainder theorem gives an expression for the remainder which we can then analyst to see if it converges to \(0\). The only difficulty is that it involves the unkown \(c(x)\) so we must use some estimates.

  • Criteria for \(f\) to be analytic

This is theorem 8.14 which follows from the Lagrange remainder theorem. If says that if the \(n\)-th derivative is bounded by \(M^n\) with the same \(M\) for each \(n\) then the Taylor series converges. We used this to show that the exponential, sine and cosine functions are analytic.

Lecture 3.1 Notes

Convergence of Taylor Polynomials

  • Taylor polynomials are the \(n\)'th partial sum.
  • The series converges to \(f\) if and only if the remainder converges to \(0\).
  • The possibilities are that the series may converge to \(f(x)\), it may converge to something else or it may diverge. Each case can happen.

Lagrange Remainder Theorem

  • We used the Cauchy Mean Value Theorem to prove the Lagrange Remainder Theorem.
  • The Cauchy Mean Value Theorem can be thought of as a "higher order" mean value theorem and is proven by induction.
  • Remember, we get an expression for the remainder but it involves an unknown function \(c(x)\).

Criteria for \(f\) to be analytic

  • This follows easily from the Lagrange remainder theorem since it allows us to bound the remainder even though we don't know what \(c(x)\) is.
  • The trigonometric and exponential functions are easily shown to satisfy the theorem and hence are analytic.
