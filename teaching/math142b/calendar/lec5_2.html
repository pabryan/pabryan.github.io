Lecture 5.2 Overview

  • Weirstrass criteria for uniform convergence It's not always easy to tell whether a sequence is uniformly convergent or not. The Weirstrass criteria says that a sequence of functions is uniformly convergent to some limit function if and only if it is a Cauchy sequence. Compare with the corresponding statement about sequences of real numbers.
  • Uniform limits of integrable functions are integrable We've see that pointwise convergence of a sequence of integrable functions is not enough to ensure the limit is integrable. Even if the limit is integrable, from pointwise convergence alone, we cannot conclude that the integral of the limit is equal to the limit of the integrals. If we replace pointwise convergence by uniform convergence, then the limit is integrable and the integral of the limit is equal to the limit of the integrals.
  • Uniform limits of differentiable functions are differentiable The situation with differentiation is a little more subtle. Once again, pointwise convergence of a sequence of differentiable functions does not ensure that the limit is differentiable. Even if the limit is differentiable, the derivative of the limit need to be equal to the limit of the derivatives. Unlike the situation with integration, to ensure that the limit is differentiable, we require that the sequence \(\{f_n\}\) is pointwise convergent to some function \(f\) and that the derived sequence \(\{f_n'\}\) is uniformly convergent to some function \(g\). In this case, then \(f' = g\) and so \(\lim_{n\to\infty} f_n' = g = f' = (\lim_{n\to\infty} f_n)'\).
