Lecture 4.3 Overview

  • Uniform Convergence The notion of uniform convergence is fudamental to the study of limits of sequences of functions. Loosely speaking, it says that the rate of convergence of \(f_n\) is the same for all \(x\). Compare this with the difference between continuity and uniform continuity.
  • Examples We will re-examine our examples and see if they are uniformly convergent.
  • Limit of uniformly continous functions is continuous We know that pointwise convergence of continuous functions is not enough to ensure the limit is continuous. Howerver, uniform convergence is sufficient.
  • Weirstrass Criteria for Uniform Convergence It's not always easy to tell whether a sequence is uniformly convergent or not. The Weirstrass criteria says that a sequence of functions is uniformly convergent to some limit function if and only if it is a Cauchy sequence. Compare with the corresponding statement about sequences of real numbers.

Lecture 4.3 Notes

Uniform Convergence

  • We looked at why the pointwise limit of continuous functions is not continuous. We discovered that the problem is that although for each \(n\), we can find a \(\delta\) in the definition of continuity, this \(\delta\) depends on \(n\) and so we can't take the limit \(n\to\infty\).
  • Letting \(\delta = \inf \{\delta(n): n \in \mathbb{N}\}\), we saw that if \(\delta > 0\), then the limit is continuous.
  • For the example \(x^n\), we saw that \(\delta(n) \to 0\) as \(n\to\infty\) which explains why the limit is not continuous.
  • This discovery lead us to the notion of equicontinuous sequences (my apologies about the confusing name I made up in class: uniformly continuous sequences of continuous functions - I couldn't remember the correct name at the time). The important property of such sequences is that \(\delta\) works for every \(n\).
  • Definition A sequence \(f_n : D\to\mathbb{R}\) of functions is called equicontinuous if for every \(x_0\in D\) and every \(\epsilon>0\), there exists a \(\delta = \delta(x_0, \epsilon)>0\) such that \[ \forall n \vert x - x_0 \vert < \delta \Rightarrow \vert f_n(x) - f_n(x_0) \vert < \epsilon. \]
  • There is another situation described in the text book where the limit is continuous. This is the case of a uniformly convergent sequence. See the text for the definition. The difference with pointwise, is that with pointwise, for each \(x\) there is a \(N=N(x,\epsilon)\), but with uniform convergence the same \(N\) works for every \(x\); that is, \(N = N(\epsilon)\).
  • Graphically, we saw that for any given \(\epsilon\) this means that \(f_n\) lies entirely in the \(\epsilon\) strip about \(f\) as long as \(n\geq N(\epsilon)\). For pointwise convergence, the \(f_n(x)\) is within \(\epsilon\) of \(f(x)\) for each \(x\), but \(f_n\) need not lie entirely within the \(\epsilon\) strip. That is, it is only with \(\epsilon\) of \(f\) at the single point \(x\).

Examples

  • We saw that \(x^n\) is not an equicontinuous sequence. It also cannot be uniformly convergent since it must jump out of the \(\epsilon\) strip at some point \(x<1\).

Limit of uniformly continous functions is continuous

  • We used the triangle inequality to prove that the limit of a uniformly convergent sequence of continuous functions is itself continuous.
  • I realised later that my proof was not quite correct. We need to first choose \(N\) so that \(\vert F_N(x_0) - F_n(x)\vert < \epsilon/3\) whenever \(\vert x - x_0\vert < \delta\). Then for any such \(x\), since \(f_n\) is uniformly convergent we have \begin{align*} \vert f(x) - f_n(x) \vert &< \epsilon/3 \\ \vert f(x_0) - f_n(x_0) \vert &< \epsilon/3. \end{align*} The reason we need uniform convergence here is that we need these inequalities not for just a fixed \(x\), but for every \(x\) such that \(\vert x - x_0\vert < \delta\). This is the step that fails for just pointwise convergence.
    • Then the triangle inequality completes the proof just as in the lecture.
