Lecture 5.3 Overview

  • Power series Power series are infinite sums, which are formally defined as the limit of the partial sums. Since they are defined as a limit, we need to consider when the limit exists. The set of \(x\) for which the limit exists is the domain of convergence of the power series. For all other \(x\), the power series diverges. It turns out that there is always a number \(r\geq 0\), called the radius of convergence, such that the power series converges for all \(x \in (x_0-r, x_0+r)\). The power series may or may not converge at the endpoints.
  • Uniform convergence of power series Power series are special in that, if the converge, they do so uniformly. That is, the sequence of partial sums converges uniformly. The way to see this is to show that the sequence of partial sums in Cauchy and apply the Weirstrass criterion. Moveover, where the power series converges it is infinitely differentiable!
  • Term-by-term operations Using the results of the previous section, we can see that to integrate or differentiate a power series, we can integrate or differentiate each partial sum and take the limit. This corresponds to performing these operations term-by-term, which is easy since each term is of the form \(a_k x^k\). This allows us to define the exponential and trigonometric functions in terms of power series.
