MATH20C Midterm 1

Instructions

  1. You may use one page of notes (written on both sides), but no books or other assistance during this exam.
  2. Write your Name, PID, and Section on the front of your Blue Book.
  3. Write your solutions clearly in your Blue Book
    1. Carefully indicate the number and letter of each question and question part.
    2. Present your answers in the same order they appear in the exam.
    3. Start each question on a new page.
  4. Read each question carefully, and answer each question completely.
  5. Show all of your work; no credit will be given for unsupported answers.
  6. The long questions require full proofs, the short answer questions only require brief justification.

Some Trigonmetric Formulae

You may find some of the following formulae useful:

  • \(\cos \pi/6 = \sqrt{3}/2\)
  • \(\cos \pi/3 = 1/2\)
  • \(\sin \pi/6 = 1/2\)
  • \(\sin \pi/3 = \sqrt{3}/2\)
  • \(\cos 5/6 \pi = - \sqrt{3}/2\)
  • \(\cos 2/3 \pi = - 1/2\)
  • \(\sin 5/6 \pi = 1/2\)
  • \(\sin 2/3 \pi = \sqrt{3}/2\)

Long Questions

These questions require full proofs.

  1. (5 points) Find the intersection of the lines \begin{align*} \mathbf{r}_1(t) &= \langle -1, 1 \rangle + t \langle 2, 3 \rangle \\ \mathbf{r}_2(s) &= \langle 4, 1 \rangle + s \langle -1, 6 \rangle. \end{align*}
  2. (5 points) Find the area of the parallelogram spanned by vectors \(\mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\) such that \(\|\mathbf{v}\| = \|\mathbf{w}\| = \sqrt{2}\) and \(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{w} = 1\).
  3. (5 points) Find a parametrisation for the intersection of the surfaces \begin{align*} z &= x^2 - y^2, \\ z &= x^2 + xy - 1. \end{align*} You may give this parametrisation using either methods discussed in the lectures and text.
  4. (5 points) For the following, you may use the rules for differentiating vector valued functions as described in the lectures and text.
    1. Show that \(\left(\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{r}\right)' = \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{r}'\) for any constant vector \(\mathbf{a}\).
    2. Let \(\mathbf{r}(t)\) be the position of an object of mass \(m\) at time \(t\). The momentum is defined as \(\mathbf{p}(t) = m \mathbf{r}'(t)\). If \(\mathbf{F}\) is the force acting on the object, then Newton's second law states that \(\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{p}'(t)\).

      For rotating objects, define the angular momentum, \(\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{r}(t) \times \mathbf{p}(t)\) and torque \(\mathbf{\tau} = \mathbf{r}(t) \times \mathbf{F}\). Using Newton's second law show that \[ \mathbf{J}' = \mathbf{\tau}. \] This is the analogue of Newton's law for rotational motion.

Short Answer (5 points total)

These questions only require brief justification.

  1. (1 point) Suppose that \(\mathbf{v}\) is a vector based at the origin \((0, 0)\), with components \(\langle 0, 1\rangle\). How, if at all, do the components change if you translate \(\mathbf{v}\) to be based at \((1,1)\)?
  2. (1 point) Which of the following is equal to \(\cos\theta\), where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\)?
    1. \(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}\)
    2. \(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{e_v}\)
    3. \(\mathbf{e_u} \cdot \mathbf{e_v}\)

    where \(\mathbf{e_u} = \mathbf{u}/\|\mathbf{u}\|\) is the unit vector pointing in the direction \(\mathbf{u}\) and similarly for \(\mathbf{e_v}\).

  3. (1 point) Suppose that a plane \(\mathcal{P}\) with normal vector \(\mathbf{n}\) and a line \(\mathcal{L}\) with direction vector \(\mathbf{v}\) both pass through the origin and that \(\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0\). Which of the following statements is correct?
    1. \(\mathcal{L}\) is contained in \(\mathcal{P}\).
    2. \(\mathcal{L}\) is orthogonal to \(\mathcal{P}\).
  4. (1 point) Which of the following does not parametrise a line?
    1. \(\mathbf{r}_1(t) = \langle 8-t, 2t, 3t\rangle\)
    2. \(\mathbf{r}_2(t) = t^3\mathbf{i} - 7t^3\mathbf{j} + t^3\mathbf{k}\)
    3. \(\mathbf{r}_3(t) = \langle 8-4t^3, 2 + 5t^2, 9t^3\rangle\)
  5. (1 point) True or false? The derivative of a vector-valued function is the slope of the tangent line, just as in the scalar-valued case.
