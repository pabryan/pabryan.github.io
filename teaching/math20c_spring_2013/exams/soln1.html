Long Questions

These questions require full proofs.

  1. (5 points) Find the intersection of the lines \begin{align*} \mathbf{r}_1(t) &= \langle -1, 1 \rangle + t \langle 2, 3 \rangle \\ \mathbf{r}_2(s) &= \langle 4, 1 \rangle + s \langle -1, 6 \rangle. \end{align*} \begin{framed} The intersection is given by points $(t,s)$ such that \[ \mathbf{r}_1(t) = \mathbf{r}_2 (s), \] or in components, \begin{align*} -1 + 2t &= 4 - s \\ 1 + 3t &= 1 + 6s. \end{align*} That is, $(s,t)$ be a solution of the linear systems of two equations in two unkowns \begin{align*} 2t + s &= 5 \\ 3t - 6s &= 0. \end{align*} The first equation gives $t=2s$ which upon substitution into the second equation gives $5s = 5$. Thus the unique solution is $(2, 1)$. \end{framed}
  2. (5 points) Find the area of the parallelogram spanned by vectors \(\mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\) such that \(\|\mathbf{v}\| = \|\mathbf{w}\| = \sqrt{2}\) and \(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{w} = 1\).

    \begin{framed} The area $A$ is given by the formula \[ A = \|\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w}\|. \] To compute this, first note that the angle $\theta$ can be determined from \[ \cos \theta = \frac{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{w}}{\|\mathbf{v}\| \|\mathbf{w}\|} = \frac{1}{2}. \] Using the formulae provided, \[ \theta = \pi/3. \] Now the area is \[ A = \|\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w}\| = \sin \theta \|\mathbf{v}\| \|\mathbf{w}\| = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \sqrt{2} \sqrt{2} = \sqrt{3}, \] once more using the formulae provided. \end{framed}

  3. (5 points) Find a parametrisation for the intersection of the surfaces \begin{align*} z &= x^2 - y^2, \\ z &= x^2 + xy - 1. \end{align*} You may give this parametrisation using either methods discussed in the lectures and text.

    There are two options here.

    \begin{framed} The first option is to find the points $(x,y)$ such that \[ x^2 - y^2 = x^2 + xy - 1. \] Cancelling the $x^2$ term are rearranging gives, \[ xy + y^2 = 1 \] which is equivalent to \[ x = \frac{1 - y^2}{y} \] for $y\ne 0$. Notice that $xy + y^2 = 1$ cannot be satisfied if $y=0$ so we can assume that $y\ne 0$. Then using the first equation (we could also use the second equation) for $z$ we have \[ \begin{split} z &= x^2 - y^2 \\ &= \left(\frac{1 - y^2}{y}\right)^2 - y^2 \\ &= \frac{1 - 2y^2 + y^4}{y^2} - \frac{y^4}{y^2} \\ &= \frac{1 - 2y^2}{y^2}. \end{split} \] Thus we can parametrise the intersection by $y$: \[ \vec{c} (y) = \langle \frac{1 - y^2}{y}, y, \frac{1-2y^2}{y^2}\rangle. \] \end{framed}

\begin{framed} The second option is to parametrise the level curves of $f(x,y) = x^2 - y^2$. This can be done as follows: \begin{align*} \vec{c}_+(t) &= \langle t, \sqrt{t^2 - c}\rangle, \quad t^2 - c \geq 0 \\ \vec{c}_-(t) &= \langle t, -\sqrt{t^2 - c}\rangle, \quad t^2 - c \geq 0. \end{align*} Then the first surface is parametrised by \[ (t, \sqrt{t^2 - s}, s), \qquad (t, -\sqrt{t^2 - s}, s) \] with $t^2 \geq s$ and any $s$. Now substitue this into the equation \[ z &= x^2 + xy - 1 \] to get \[ s = t^2 \pm t \sqrt{t^2 - s} - 1 \] or \[ (s^2 - t^2 + 1)^2 = t^2(t^2 - s) \] At this point, I would just give up and try the other way! I leave it to those interested to complete it from here solving for $s$ as function of $t$ or vice versa. \end{framed} \begin{framed} There is in fact, a third option, which is to use \emph{hyperbolic polar} coordinates (if you aren't familar with hyperbolic trigonmetric functions, just ignore this option!). This method is preferential to the second method as it gives a better parametrisation (try for instance doing $z=x^2+y^2$ using the second method as compared with using polar coordinates). Hyperbolic polar coordinates give \[ x = r \cosh t \qquad y = r \sinh t \qquad z = r^2. \] Now proceed as you would for $z = x^2 + y^2$ in polar coordinates \[ x = r \cos t \qquad y = r \sin t \qquad z = r^2. \] \end{framed}

  1. (5 points) For the following, you may use the rules for differentiating vector valued functions as described in the lectures and text.
    1. Show that \(\left(\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{r}\right)' = \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{r}'\) for any constant vector \(\mathbf{a}\).

      \begin{framed} \[ \left(\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{r}\right)' = \mathbf{a}' \times \mathbf{r} + \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{r}' = \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{r}' \] since $\mathbf{a}' = \mathbf{0}$. \end{framed}

    2. Let \(\mathbf{r}(t)\) be the position of an object of mass \(m\) at time \(t\). The momentum is defined as \(\mathbf{p}(t) = m \mathbf{r}'(t)\). If \(\mathbf{F}\) is the force acting on the object, then Newton's second law states that \(\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{p}'(t)\).

      For rotating objects, define the angular momentum, \(\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{r}(t) \times \mathbf{p}(t)\) and torque \(\mathbf{\tau} = \mathbf{r}(t) \times \mathbf{F}\). Using Newton's second law show that \[ \mathbf{J}' = \mathbf{\tau}. \] This is the analogue of Newton's law for rotational motion.

      \begin{framed} Differentiate $\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{r}(t) \times \mathbf{p}(t)$ to obtain \[ \mathbf{J}' = (\mathbf{r}' \times \mathbf{p}) + (\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}'). \] Since $\mathbf{p} = m \mathbf{r}'$ we have \[ \mathbf{r}' \times \mathbf{p} = m (\mathbf{r}' \times \mathbf{r}') = \mathbf{0}. \] Then since $\mathbf{p}' = \mathbf{F}$ we have \[ \mathbf{J}' = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}' = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{\tau}. \] \end{framed}

Short Answer (5 points total)

These questions only require brief justification.

  1. (1 point) Suppose that \(\mathbf{v}\) is a vector based at the origin \((0, 0)\), with components \(\langle 0, 1\rangle\). How, if at all, do the components change if you translate \(\mathbf{v}\) to be based at \((1,1)\)?

    \begin{framed} They don't change at all! The components are computed with respect to the base point. \end{framed}

  2. (1 point) Which of the following is equal to \(\cos\theta\), where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\)?
    1. \(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}\)
    2. \(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{e_v}\)
    3. \(\mathbf{e_u} \cdot \mathbf{e_v}\)

    where \(\mathbf{e_u} = \mathbf{u}/\|\mathbf{u}\|\) is the unit vector pointing in the direction \(\mathbf{u}\) and similarly for \(\mathbf{e_v}\).

    \begin{framed} (c). The other options don't take into account the length of the vectors involved. \end{framed}

  1. (1 point) Suppose that a plane \(\mathcal{P}\) with normal vector \(\mathbf{n}\) and a line \(\mathcal{L}\) with direction vector \(\mathbf{v}\) both pass through the origin and that \(\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0\). Which of the following statements is correct?
    1. \(\mathcal{L}\) is contained in \(\mathcal{P}\).
    2. \(\mathcal{L}\) is orthogonal to \(\mathcal{P}\).

    \begin{framed} (a) The vector $\vec{v}$ lies in the plane $\mathcal{P}$ since the plane is composed of all vectors based at the origin and orthogonal to $\vec{n}$. Thus the line also lies in the plane. \end{framed}

  2. (1 point) Which of the following does not parametrise a line?
    1. \(\mathbf{r}_1(t) = \langle 8-t, 2t, 3t\rangle\)
    2. \(\mathbf{r}_2(t) = t^3\mathbf{i} - 7t^3\mathbf{j} + t^3\mathbf{k}\)
    3. \(\mathbf{r}_3(t) = \langle 8-4t^3, 2 + 5t^2, 9t^3\rangle\)

    \begin{framed} (c) This not of the form $\vec{p} + t \vec{v}$. Note that setting $s=t^3$, option b is of the form $\vec{p} + s \vec{v}$. \end{framed}

  3. (1 point) True or false? The derivative of a vector-valued function is the slope of the tangent line, just as in the scalar-valued case.

    \begin{framed} False. The derivative is the tangent vector. \end{framed}
